# Scaling People: Deep Dive — Claire Hughes Johnson

Source: "Scaling People: Tactics for Management and Company Building" (2023, Stripe Press)
Author: Claire Hughes Johnson (COO Stripe 2014-2021, VP Google 10 years, HBS faculty)
Research date: 2026-03-03

---

## Book Structure

The book is organized around four essential operating principles and four core frameworks, plus a final chapter on self-management ("You"). It includes a 120-page supplement of templates, worksheets, and exercises. The structure:

1. **Essential Operating Principles** (Chapter 1)
2. **Core Framework 1**: Foundations and Planning for Goals and Resources (Chapter 2)
3. **Core Framework 2**: A Comprehensive Hiring Approach (Chapter 3)
4. **Core Framework 3**: Intentional Team Development (Chapter 4)
5. **Core Framework 4**: Feedback and Performance Mechanisms (Chapter 5)
6. **You** — Self-management for managers (final chapter)

The four essential operating principles:
- Build self-awareness to build mutual awareness
- Say the thing you think you cannot say
- Distinguish between management and leadership
- Come back to the operating system

## 1. Operating Frameworks

### The Operating System Concept

Johnson frames all management infrastructure as an "operating system" — a repeatable, transferable structure that every team she manages shares the same components:

- Clear missions
- Stated goals
- Metrics that matter
- Similar meeting structures
- Weekly and quarterly cadences

**Key insight**: "Good systems create velocity." The operating system is not bureaucracy — it is the mechanism that allows speed at scale. Process is not the enemy of agility; lack of process is.

### Founding Documents

Every company needs foundational documents that rarely change:

| Document | Purpose | Change frequency |
|----------|---------|-----------------|
| **Mission** | Why you exist. Specific, aspirational, never done. Flows down: company → division → team | Rarely |
| **Long-term goals** | 3-5 year direction | Annually reviewed |
| **Operating principles** (values) | How you behave and work | Refreshed annually, rarely change substantively |
| **Team charters** | Per-team mission, scope, goals | Per team formation/reorg |

Operating principles should acknowledge tensions between themselves. Example: "think rigorously" vs "move with urgency" — context determines which takes precedence. They should NOT be a toothless list of nice-to-haves.

These documents should be:
- Stored centrally, easily referenced
- Core to new employee onboarding
- Baked into hiring rubrics and performance reviews
- Treated as a rubric for assessing cultural fit

### Operating Cadence

Johnson prescribes a specific operating cadence with an owner for each element:

| Cadence | Frequency | Purpose |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Annual planning** | Yearly | Goal-setting, resource allocation, strategy |
| **Quarterly business reviews (QBRs)** | Quarterly | Progress against goals, course correction |
| **Monthly all-hands** | Monthly | Company-wide alignment, celebrations |
| **Biweekly 1:1s** | Biweekly (or weekly) | Manager-report relationship, coaching |
| **Weekly snippets** | Weekly | Individual status updates (written) |
| **Weekly metrics reviews** | Weekly | Data review, trend spotting |
| **Weekly team meetings** | Weekly | Update + decision-making/work review |
| **Daily standups** | Daily | Coordination, blockers |

**Solicit frequent feedback on the operating system and cadence.** Key diagnostic questions:
- Does everyone know the mission and objectives for the company, division, and team?
- Does everyone know the work they're individually expected to do?
- Do people know how decisions are made?

### Goals and Metrics

**Good goals should be**:
- Easy for everyone to memorize
- Focused on outcomes, not activities (don't start with a verb — state an end state)
- Objective and assessable
- Credible but stretch
- User-oriented, not function-oriented
- Allow for autonomy in how they're achieved

**Goals define success. Start from the end in mind.** Focus on top priorities. Beware of the "Pyrrhic victory" — achieving goals in an unsustainable way.

**Metrics that matter**:
- Company targets (financial outputs)
- Important inputs (leading indicators)
- "Zero targets" (things like outages that should never happen)
- Employee engagement surveys
- Long-term + short-term + operating/input metrics

Every metric should have an owner on the exec team. Dashboard for metrics reviewed weekly or biweekly.

### Process Philosophy

- Watch out for defensive / "cover your ass" processes
- Make ownership and accountability clear
- Lightweight checks preferred over heavyweight gates
- Beware of stale processes — audit meetings every 6 months, poll participants
- Allow for experimentation within the system
- Process does NOT mean meetings — project trackers, email, async docs count

### Internal Communications

- Commit important information to company docs, not just verbal
- Meeting notes should be shared
- After 150 people, you need an explicit internal comms strategy
- Long-form writing: pre-reads and memos stored in a public wiki
- Distinguish between evergreen documents and one-time communications
- Document style guide and writing classes
- Communicate important info at least **three times in different ways**
- Communicate MORE in times of crisis or change

## 2. Hiring and Team Building

### Recruiting Pipeline

- Educate candidates about company existence (brand building)
- Mine networks of existing employees (referral programs)
- Write blog posts that attract candidates
- Run coding challenges / work sample tests to find applicants
- Strong recruiter and recruiting logistics
- At Stripe: dispensed with whiteboard programming, de-emphasized credentials, actively combated unconscious bias, introduced work sample tests

### Job Ladder and Levels

Johnson advocates for clear job ladders with level expectations:

| Level | Experience | Characteristics |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| Level 1 (Entry) | 0-1 year | Gets full guidance |
| Level 2 (Developing) | 1-2 years | Gets some guidance |
| Level 3 (Career) | 3-4 years | Works independently, complex work |
| Level 4 (Advanced) | 8-10 years | Could be manager, coordinates others |
| Level 5 (Expert) | 15+ years | Knows more than anyone, supervises |
| Level 6 (Principal) | Senior | Cross-organizational influence |

Based on level, establish salary band and target offer point.

### Interview Process

**Candidate selection**:
- Candidate review for job level and hire/no-hire decision
- Consistency and transparency in evaluation
- Bias control mechanisms
- Defined roles on the candidate review team (not everyone interviews for the same signals)

**Johnson's key interview questions**:
1. **Agency test**: "Tell me about a project you were involved in that failed." Look for: Does the person show agency? Do they say "here's what I could have done differently" (green flag) or "the scope was wrong, my teammate didn't pull their weight" (red flag — no agency, doesn't believe they affect outcomes)?
2. **Curiosity/learning test**: Does the candidate orient toward learning and getting better?
3. **Self-awareness**: Johnson says self-awareness is present in only 10-15% of people. It's the #1 skill she looks for.

**Hiring rubrics**: Consistent rubrics mitigate demographic bias. Standardized scoring applies identically to all applicants. The hiring manager decides but only after listening to the hiring committee's views — collaborative decision-making with clear accountability.

**Reference calls**: Johnson does them personally. Key reference question: "I'm going to be their manager. What can I do to help them be successful?" This is "extremely revealing" because the reference goes into manager mode and describes the person's blind spots, performance issues, and development needs.

**Candidate experience metrics**: Sentiment analysis and NPS from candidates.

### Onboarding

**Company onboarding should include**:
- The "why" of the company (mission, origin story)
- The "what" of the business (current priorities, goals)
- The "how" of company behaviors (operating principles, how people work together)

**Manager onboarding with new report** (first conversation topics):
1. **Onboarding context**: Share team context, information
2. **Operating approach**: How you'll work together, when you'll meet, what you'll talk about
3. **Management style**: What kind of manager you are, how you'll be involved. Share your "Working with Me" guide. Invite them to write their own
4. **Communication preferences**: Preferred channels, response time expectations, how to use 1:1 time
5. **Initial priorities**: Team's work priorities and individual's role priorities
6. **Career conversation**: "Explain that once you've worked together for a few months, you'll schedule time for a longer meeting to learn more about the person's experience, the arc of their career, and their ambitions for the future"

**New Leader Experience (NLE)** at Stripe — a separate onboarding track for leaders:
- Welcome emails
- Pre-scheduled meetings and pre-reads
- Hogan Personality Inventory (leadership assessment)
- Access to an executive coach for 6 months
- Suggested first-month actions
- 360-degree review at end of first 90 days for immediate feedback
- Dedicated training on Stripe's operating principles (critical because "managers bring whatever rules and behaviors they had in their prior roles — and they have tremendous influence on their teams")

## 3. Feedback and Performance Management

### Feedback Philosophy

**Core principle**: No one should be surprised by their feedback. Don't wait too long to offer coaching.

Johnson distinguishes between ongoing informal feedback and formal review processes, and argues you need BOTH — especially in young companies where managers are inexperienced:

> "Ideally, feedback should be continuous, but in young companies, many managers are new to this process, so they're not giving as much feedback. Therefore, you actually have to build more structures for it, which may seem counterintuitive."

### The "Explorer, Not Lecturer" Framework for Hard Feedback

This is Johnson's signature feedback method. The core shift: **separate the person from the work**.

> "Too often when we're about to give feedback, we feel like we're judging a person. I'm not saying 'Brett, you're a bad communicator.' I'm judging the work, the task."

**The Left-Hand Column Method**:
Every conversation has a "left-hand column" — the running internal commentary that is often harsh, judgmental, or cynical. Before speaking, you must "detoxify" this column.

**Steps**:
1. **Be an Explorer, not a Lecturer**: Don't approach as an expert delivering a verdict. Act as a partner in discovery
2. **Make the Implicit Explicit**: Surface what everyone is thinking but not saying ("It feels like there's something we're not saying. It feels tense in here.")
3. **State observations, not judgments**: "I noticed your last two projects missed their deadlines by two weeks. Is there something I should help with or something I should know?"
4. **Lead with curiosity**: Ask "How did you think that went?" before giving your view
5. **Share an empathetic observation paired with a question**

**Practical technique**: From Fred Kofman's "Conscious Business" training — detoxify your left-hand column and actually say it, constructively.

**Key coaching move** for early-career reports: When they blame others ("Lucy didn't send me her notes"), ask "What could you have done differently?" Then sit in the uncomfortable silence. Let them arrive at agency on their own.

### Hypothesis-Based Coaching

A three-step inquisitive coaching method:

1. **Observe** your employees carefully over time
2. **Identify** persistent shortcomings (and strengths) — look for patterns
3. **Share** findings tactfully, framed as hypotheses to be tested together, not verdicts

> "I'm not telling you what I think is true. I'm sharing a hypothesis based on what I've observed, and I'd like to test it with you."

### Formal Performance Reviews

**Elements of a strong review process**:
1. **Performance feedback** (written review)
2. **Calibration** (cross-team comparison)
3. **Compensation structures** (tied to performance)

**Process flow**:
1. Peer feedback and employee self-assessment submitted simultaneously
2. Manager review written AFTER reading peer and self-assessment feedback
3. Calibration across managers/teams
4. Review conversation with employee

**Key reframe**: "Written performance reviews are, by nature, backward-looking, but they're most productive when you use them to set the stage for a discussion about the future." Redirect backward-looking comments to forward-looking discussion.

**What you need for trust in the system**:
- A talent strategy (e.g., "pay for performance")
- Rubrics for how performance is measured
- A program to capture performance feedback and promotion nominations
- A clearly communicated calibration process
- A compensation philosophy that connects performance to outcomes

### Calibration Process

Steps for calibration:
1. Establish job ladder with expectations per level
2. Create rubric with assessment criteria for meeting expectations at each level (with examples)
3. Managers present their assessments to peer managers
4. Cross-comparison for consistency and fairness
5. Calibration should surface cases where managers are rating too high or too low

### Managing the Talent Portfolio

Johnson treats each team as a **portfolio of talent** with three segments:

**High performers**: Before promoting, have them do the duties of the next role first. "It's very easy to promote people too quickly." Do a gut check: are they truly performing at the next level and can they sustain it?

**The steady middle**: Easy to neglect. "Particularly unfortunate because they're often sources of stability, and many are culture carriers." Every person should be assessed and either supported, coached to better performance, or moved to a more suitable role.

**Low performers**: Don't wait for a PIP to address issues. Start coaching as soon as you see problems. PIPs should never be a surprise — they should be the culmination of ongoing feedback conversations that have been documented.

### Promotions

- Before promoting someone, have them perform the duties of the new role to see how they do
- Don't promote too quickly — verify sustained performance at the next level
- Compensation tied to behavior and results promotes fairness

## 4. 1:1 Meetings

### Structure and Cadence

- **Frequency**: Biweekly or weekly, consistent times so the report can plan
- **Documentation**: Joint 1:1 document to track agendas, actions, goals, and updates (shared Google Doc or equivalent)
- **Purpose**: Items better discussed verbally that can wait for the weekly check-in. Not for things that need immediate attention (use Slack/email for those)

### Content Categories

Johnson's 1:1s cover:

1. **Work check-ins**: Project status, blockers, progress on goals
2. **Personal check-ins**: How the person is doing, energy levels, concerns
3. **Coaching and feedback**: Observations, hypothesis-based coaching, development conversations
4. **Decision support**: When reports bring decisions, default response is "What do you want to do?" or "What should you do?" — help them decide, don't decide for them
5. **Big decisions awareness**: "If there is a big decision brewing, I'd love to know about it, and I'm always here to talk it out"

### Meeting Norms

From her "Working with Claire" document:
- Use 1:1s for items better discussed verbally
- Email takes a LOT of time — use it wisely
- If you don't have a 1:1 for a while, feel free to email or ping
- Action items are taken seriously — know what yours are, when they're due, get them done
- Fine to renegotiate deadlines, but not the day after the deadline

### Career Conversations

Separate from regular 1:1s. Scheduled after a few months of working together. Longer meeting to learn about:
- The person's experience
- The arc of their career
- Their ambitions for the future

### Skip-Level and Pulse Meetings

Johnson explicitly does skip-levels, office hours, etc. to maintain pulse on the broader team:

> "I also like to know how and what your team is thinking and feeling and I will do skip levels, office hours, etc. Remember, whatever I hear or see, I have your back and I'll tell you when I'm concerned. Anyone who vents to me about you is going to get my help to tell you directly."

## 5. Decision-Making Frameworks

### The Impact/Reversibility Matrix

Johnson's primary delegation and decision-making framework, similar to Bezos's Type 1 / Type 2 decisions. Two axes:

| | High Impact | Low Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Irreversible (Trapdoor)** | Leader decides with rigor, data, many stakeholders | Delegate with guidance |
| **Reversible (Adjustable)** | Collaborative decision, leader aware | Individual empowerment, move fast |

**High impact + irreversible** (trapdoor): Requires lots of data, rigor, more stakeholders, maybe multiple teams. Example: pricing changes.

**Low impact + reversible**: Individual applies best judgment based on operating principles. Examples: UI changes, customer service responses.

**Key insight**: Most projects can be plotted on this 2x2. Based on where they land, you know better how to proceed.

### Decision Types

Johnson identifies five decision-making modes:

1. **Autocratic**: One decision-maker. May consult the team, but they're the decider
2. **Consensus**: Everyone in the group agrees together
3. **Democratic**: Group votes, majority wins
4. **Consultative**: Leader consults the group but ultimately decides
5. **Delegated**: Leader and team agree on a delegate who decides; all stand behind that person's decision

### Decision Documentation

- Keep a **decision log** of all past decisions
- Be explicit about HOW each type of decision will be made
- Map decision-making authority to org structure
- Write "roles and responsibilities" docs that clarify what each person is the primary decision-maker for, and where to go if they're not

**Recommended frameworks she references**:
- **SPADE** (Setting, People, Alternatives, Decide, Explain) — Gokul Rajaram / Square
- **RAPID** — Bain
- **McKinsey's seven-step problem-solving process**

### Decision-Making Principles at Scale

> "Otherwise, it just starts defaulting to the founder, which is not healthy for scale."

**Johnson's personal decision-making style** (from Working with Claire):
- "I expect you are making decisions a lot without me"
- When reports bring decisions: "I'll usually put it back on you with 'What do you want to do?' or 'What should you do?' and help you decide"
- Wants to know about big decisions brewing
- Likes to agree on HOW things are done as a group, then change or make exceptions as needed
- Favors data in decision-making but acknowledges going into "intuitive mode" sometimes

## 6. Communication Practices

### Team Meetings

Johnson views team meetings as **both update and decision-making/work-review forums**.

**Meeting roles (rotated)**:
- DRI (Directly Responsible Individual)
- Facilitator
- Note taker

**Meeting norms to establish**:
- Logistics (time, on-time arrival)
- Delegation policy (can someone send a delegate?)
- Pre-reads (complete before meeting or reading time at start?)
- Action items (updated async or at meeting start?)
- Electronics use (laptops closed except for presenter/notetaker?)
- "No topics undiscussable"
- Purpose, agenda, time limit for each meeting/session
- Disagree and commit mechanism
- Parking lot for off-topic items with a process to review
- Action items with owners and timelines

**Check-in / Check-out practice**:
- Ask each person for a quick work check-in and personal check-in at start
- "What's 1 thing you'll take away from this meeting?"
- One-word checkout for how feeling

**Meeting hygiene**: Audit meetings every 6 months. Poll participants. Distinguish between operator/manager mode meetings and creative maker mode meetings.

### All-Hands / Company-Wide Meetings

- Use sparingly as company grows
- Celebrate things
- Review key metrics and goals publicly

### Remote Work Communication

- More documentation required
- Give equal footing to remote participants
- Shared Slack channels for visibility
- Record meetings
- Force everyone to join video calls from separate locations (to equalize remote/in-person)
- Make room for in-person time quarterly
- Respect international differences in work culture

### Written Communication

- Long-form writing preferred for complex topics (memos > slide decks)
- Pre-reads stored in a public wiki/site
- Internal site to discover information and learn about each other
- Style guide for written communications
- Assess internal comms on: Complete, Accessible, Reliable, Accurate, Transparent

**Johnson's writing rule**: "Communicate important information at least three times in different ways." In crisis or change, communicate even more.

### Praise and Criticism

- Praise publicly, criticize privately
- BUT: criticizing ideas is different from criticizing behavior — ideas can be challenged in group settings
- Quick feedback preferred — don't wait

## 7. Self-Management for Managers

This is the final chapter of the book ("You"), which Johnson considers crucial.

### Energy Mapping

**Tactic: Calendar marking for good/bad days.**

> "Diagnose what gives and takes your energy. Map out your good and bad days and track what activities add to and detract from your energy. Keep check marks on your calendar of good days and bad days. After a month, look at all the good days and bad days, and the good weeks and bad weeks, and see what trends jump out."

Johnson's personal finding: Weeks with more than one work event that kept her from dinner with her kids were bad weeks. She resolved to restrict work-related late nights to once a week.

**Goal**: Study what combination of time spent on which activities creates your best performance, then set boundaries to preserve your strongest self.

### Time Management

From her "Working with Claire" document:
- "I am often overly generous with my time and say yes too often to things. If you see this, please flag it to me."
- "Although I love meeting with people, I sometimes don't spend enough time on the strategic stuff because I am working on other things. Help keep me honest."
- Balance operator/manager meetings with strategic/creative time
- Distinguish between tasks you must do yourself (high impact + irreversible) and everything else (delegate)

### The "Working with Me" Document

Johnson's signature self-management practice. She first wrote hers in 2010 at Google and has shared every version since. Sections include:

1. **My role**: Your role and goals
2. **About me**: Personality, communication preferences, boundaries
3. **Operating approach**: Day-to-day cadence (1:1s, staff meetings, operating cadence), what to loop you in on, longer-term strategic and planning cadence, how you keep a pulse on the team
4. **Management style**: How you manage, how involved you'll be
5. **Supporting you and your team**: Career discussion expectations, development, goals, check-in on progress
6. **Decision-making**: How you like to make decisions, what information you want to see
7. **Communication preferences**: Channels, response times

**Key insight**: The document hasn't changed markedly since 2009 — which itself is a data point about core operating style stability.

**Why it matters**: "You're trying to create a shortcut, because there's anxiety when we first work with someone... Just tell them how to work with you."

Johnson recommends everyone writes one, not just managers. Share it openly, invite people to challenge it ("Yeah, you think you act this way, but you really don't").

She added a section about micromanagement after her team pointed out the gap: "Sometimes when it feels like I'm micromanaging you, it's because I'm trying to learn. The first time I've ever done a thing, you are going to see me hyper-involved, but what we should do is establish that ahead of time."

### Self-Awareness as Core Skill

Johnson's four personal operating principles:
1. **Build self-awareness to build mutual awareness** — the one she talks about most
2. **Say the thing you think you cannot say**
3. **Distinguish between management and leadership** — "Leadership is about change. Management is about stability."
4. **Come back to the operating system**

Frameworks for self-awareness: Insights Discovery, DISC, MBTI, Big Five. Understand:
- Introvert vs extrovert
- Talk to think vs think to talk
- Process-oriented vs outcome-oriented
- Task-oriented vs people-oriented

### Career Self-Assessment

Johnson picks her head up every 6 months, with deeper assessment on 12-18 month cycles:
- Am I learning?
- Am I in a role that could lead to another role?
- Am I going to be doing stuff 6 months from now that I'll be learning from and having impact with?
- Is this roughly on a trajectory that makes sense?

## 8. Coaching and Development

### Skill/Will Matrix

Johnson uses the skill/will matrix to diagnose team member performance:

| | High Skill | Low Skill |
|---|---|---|
| **High Will** | Delegate, give autonomy, stretch assignments | Train, develop, mentor |
| **Low Will** | Investigate root cause, re-engage or exit | Coach intensively or transition |

**Two skill scenarios**:
- Too little skill: Train the team member OR hire someone who already has the skill
- Too much skill (tasks bore them): Shift goals to be more demanding

### See One, Do One, Teach One

Johnson's delegation development model. Progress through stages of increasing autonomy:
1. **See one**: Watch someone else do it, learn the approach
2. **Do one**: Execute it yourself with support
3. **Teach one**: Teach someone else, cementing mastery and multiplying capability

### Coaching the Victim vs Player Mindset

From Tim Ferriss interview — Johnson tests for and coaches around agency:

**Victim orientation**: "Lucy didn't send me her notes." "The scope was wrong." "My teammate didn't pull their weight." No sense of personal agency or ability to affect outcomes.

**Player orientation**: "Here's what I could have done differently." "I learned X about what I need to do next time." Takes ownership.

**Coaching move**: When someone displays victim orientation, ask "What could you have done differently?" and sit in the uncomfortable silence. Earlier-career people are more coachable here. Later-career people with ingrained victim patterns are very hard to shift.

### Career Development Conversations

- Schedule a dedicated longer meeting after a few months of working together
- Learn about: their experience, career arc, and ambitions
- One or two individual goals for personal development alongside team goals
- Before promoting: have them perform the duties of the next role to see how they do

### Offsites for Team Development

- Shared planning document with prework
- Daily check-ins at start of each day (share candid feelings and goals for the day)
- Icebreakers: share personal photo, favorite book or concert
- Purpose, agenda, time limit, actions for each session
- Track team formation stages: forming, storming, norming, performing

## 9. Crisis Management and Difficult Conversations

### "Say the Thing You Think You Cannot Say"

Johnson's second operating principle is specifically about having difficult conversations. The full framework:

1. **Detoxify your left-hand column**: Identify the harsh internal thought, strip the judgment, leave the objective observation
2. **Name the shared goal**: "I think we both want X..."
3. **Reframe as collaborative inquiry**: "From my seat, 10,000-20,000 feet away, I have a couple of things I'm worried about" — then discuss together
4. **Read the room**: "It feels like there's something we're not saying. It feels tense in here." Then call on someone to break through to the real thing

### Managing Low Performers

- Investigate root cause with probing questions first
- Discuss how to get back on track
- Reset goals and document why
- Don't wait for PIPs — coach early and continuously
- If performance doesn't improve, be direct and timely about consequences
- Before PIP: the person should already know they're struggling from prior feedback conversations

### Team Reorgs

Johnson provides a structured approach:
- Communications plan for every reorg
- Be explicit about why the change is happening
- Address internal mobility (people running to something vs away from something vs being pushed)
- Treat internal new hires with proper onboarding too — don't assume they know everything

### Crisis Communication

- Communicate more in times of crisis or change
- Use multiple channels (3x rule: same message, three different ways)
- Make company-wide meetings count during crisis
- Be transparent about what you know and what you don't
- State the obvious — people need to hear leadership name the problem

### "Code Yellow"

Stripe practice: When an existential risk is identified, declare "Code Yellow" to quickly solve it by removing barriers and redirecting resources. A crisis escalation mechanism that breaks normal process when warranted.

### Underperforming Teams

- Investigate root cause with probing questions
- Discuss how to get back on track
- Reset goals and document why the reset happened
- Identify dependencies during planning that might be causing issues

## 10. Automatable / LLM-Assistable Practices

Based on the full analysis, here are Johnson's practices mapped to automation potential:

### High Automation Potential (LLM can do most of the work)

| Practice | What to automate | Implementation |
|----------|-----------------|----------------|
| **1:1 agenda generation** | Pre-populate from: action items from last 1:1, recent team metrics, goal progress, open decisions, upcoming deadlines | Pull from 1:1 doc, project trackers, goal systems |
| **Meeting prep** | Generate briefing docs for any meeting: attendees, context, open items, relevant metrics, previous decisions | Aggregate from calendar, docs, metrics dashboards |
| **Performance review preparation** | Aggregate peer feedback, self-assessment, goal achievement data, 1:1 notes, project outcomes into structured draft | Parse goal systems, feedback tools, 1:1 docs |
| **Decision document templates** | Generate SPADE/RAPID templates pre-filled with context, stakeholders, known alternatives | Template + context injection |
| **Weekly snippets aggregation** | Summarize team snippets into team-level status report | Parse individual snippets, synthesize |
| **Metrics dashboard commentary** | Generate narrative around metric changes, flag anomalies, suggest investigation areas | Time-series analysis + narrative generation |
| **Career conversation prep** | Generate person profile: tenure, projects, strengths observed, development areas from feedback history, career trajectory | Aggregate from HR systems, feedback history |
| **Onboarding checklist generation** | Create personalized onboarding plan based on role, level, team context | Template + role/team context |
| **Reference check question generation** | Generate tailored reference questions based on role requirements and interview signals | Role description + interview debrief notes |
| **Calibration prep** | Pre-sort team into performance tiers based on goal achievement, feedback signals, project outcomes | Quantitative data aggregation |

### Medium Automation Potential (LLM assists, human decides)

| Practice | What to automate | What stays human |
|----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Feedback drafting** | Draft observation-based feedback using Explorer framework: "I noticed [data]. Is there something I should know?" | Delivery, tone calibration, relationship context |
| **Coaching hypothesis generation** | Identify patterns from 1:1 notes, project data, peer feedback to suggest coaching hypotheses | Testing the hypothesis in conversation |
| **Working-with-me document drafting** | Generate initial draft from behavioral data, communication patterns, calendar analysis | Personal reflection, authenticity check |
| **Goal writing** | Draft outcome-oriented goals from strategy docs and team priorities | Stretch calibration, individual alignment |
| **Reorg comms plan** | Draft communication plan structure, stakeholder matrix, FAQ anticipation | Judgment calls, emotional calibration |
| **Interview rubric generation** | Generate role-specific interview rubrics from job ladder, team needs, operating principles | Signal weighting, team fit assessment |
| **Operating cadence audit** | Analyze meeting load, attendee overlap, outcome tracking to identify stale meetings | Decision to cut/keep/modify |
| **Energy mapping analysis** | Analyze calendar patterns against self-reported good/bad days to surface energy patterns | Setting personal boundaries |
| **Skill/will matrix assessment** | Aggregate signals for each team member to suggest quadrant placement | Manager judgment and calibration |
| **Decision log maintenance** | Track decisions made, outcomes, participants, and surface precedents for new decisions | Making the actual decisions |

### Low Automation Potential (Human-primary, LLM marginal)

| Practice | Why it stays human |
|----------|--------------------|
| **Difficult conversations** | Emotional attunement, real-time reading of the room, relationship stakes |
| **Firing / PIP delivery** | Legal, emotional, ethical dimensions require human judgment and presence |
| **Skip-level meetings** | Trust-building requires personal presence and confidentiality |
| **Team offsite facilitation** | Group dynamics, real-time facilitation, vulnerability |
| **Crisis leadership communication** | Authenticity, emotional presence, real-time adaptation |
| **Promotion decisions** | Portfolio balancing, organizational politics, future potential assessment |
